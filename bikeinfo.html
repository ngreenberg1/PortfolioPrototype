Bike Image map

The downtube is a part of the bicycle frame. It connects the head tube to the bottom bracket shell.

The bicycle fork is the part of a bicycle that holds the front wheel. A steerer tube attaches the fork to the bicycle and the handlebars (via the stem) allowing the rider to steer the bicycle.

The head tube is a part of the frame, and contain the headset. The headset contains bearing for the fork via its steerer tube.

The seat tube contains the seatpost of the bike, which connects to the saddle. The saddle height is adjustable by changing how far the seatpost is inserted into the seat tube.

The front derailleur moves the chain side to side between the front chainrings. It clamps to the seattube, and has a cage through which the chain passes. When the rider shifts with the fron shifter, the cage moves the chain up or down.

The rotor is a metal disc attached to the wheel hub that rotates with the wheel. The caliper squeezes the rotor to cause braking.

The top tube, or cross-bar, is part of the frame, and connects the top of the head tube to the top of the seat tube.

The bottom bracket connects the crankarms to the bicycle and allows them to rotate freely. It contains a spindle to which the crankset attaches, and the bearings that allow the spindle and crankset to rotate.

The crank arm is part of the crankset, which is the component of a bicycle drivetrain that converts the reciprocating motion of the rider's legs into rotational motion used to drive the chain or belt, which in turn drive the rear wheel.

The rear derailleur moves the chain between the cassette sprockets, and takes up chain slack caused by moving to a smaller sprocket at the rear or a smallerchainring by the front derailleur. Most rear dearailleurs consist of a a cage that holds two pulleys that guide the chain in an S-shaped pattern.

The thru axle is a removable axle with a threaded end that is inserted through a hole in one fork leg, through the hub, and then screwed into the other fork leg.

The cassette is the set of multiple sprockets that attaches to the hub on the rear wheel. The cassette works with the rear derailleur to provide multiple gear ratios to the rider.

The pedal is the part of a bicycle that the rider pushes with their foot to propel the vehicle. It provides the connection between the rider's foot or shoe and the crank allowing the leg to turn the bottom bracket spindle and propel the bicycle's wheels. Clipless pedals require a special cycling shoe with a cleat fitted to the sole, which locks into a mechanism in the pedal and thus holds the shoe firmly to the pedal.

The handlebars are the steering control for the bicycle. It is most often directly mechanically linked to a pivoting front wheel via a stem, which in turn attaches it to the fork.

The hoods act as a handrest when the rider has their hands on the brake levers.

The stem is the component on a bicycle that connects the handlebars to the steerer tube of the bicycle fork.

The seatpost is the tube that extends upward from the bicycle frame to the saddle. It is attached to the frame by inserting into the seat tube, and is held in place by the seat post clamp. The seatpost clamps to the saddle via the saddle rails.

The seatpost clamp clamps down on the seat tube, and holds the seat post in place

The seat stays are part of the frame, and connect the top of the seat tube to the rear fork dropouts.

The chain is a roller chain that transfers power from the deals to the drive-wheel of a bicycle, thus propelling it.

The chainrings engage the chain to transfer power to the rear wheel. A bicycle can have between one and three chaing rings which in combination with the cassette, allow for different gear ratios.

The water bottle cages attach to the bicycle frame via bolts which thread into eyelets, typically placed on the downtube and seattube.

The rim is the part of the wheel to which the tire attaches. The rim is attached to the hub, at the center of the wheel, with spokes.

The rim is the part of the wheel to which the tire attaches. The rim is attached to the hub, at the center of the wheel, with spokes.

The bicycle tire provides an important source of suspension, generates the lateral forces necesarry for balancing and turning, and generates the longitudinal forces necessary for propulsion and braking. Bicycle tires are typically pneumatic, with pressurized air being held inside with a seperate inner tube, or by the tire and the rim, in a tubeless system. Tire surfaces can vary, the appropriate tire is usually dependent on the surface one is riding on.

The brake and shift levers transmit the force applied by the rider through a mechanical or hydraulic mechanism. When the rider squeezes the lever, the brake is activated, and when the rider pushes the lever inward, the shifting cable is pulled, which causes the derailleur to move the chain into a different gear.

The saddle, often called a bicycle seat, is one of five contact points on an upright boicycle. The saddle is composed of three parts, a shell cover and rails. The rails connect the saddle to the seatpost.

A spoke is a tensioned, adjustable metal wire radiating from the center of the wheel (the hub where the axle connects), connecting the hub with the rim. The spoke tension keeps the wheel round, true, and in dish.

The headset is the set of componenets that provides a rotatable interface between the fork and the head tube. A typical headset consists of two cups that are pressed into the top and bottom of the headtube. Inside the two cups are bearings.
